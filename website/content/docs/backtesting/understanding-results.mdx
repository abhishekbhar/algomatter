# Understanding Backtest Results

After a backtest completes, Algomatter presents a results dashboard with charts, metrics, and a trade log. This guide explains what each component means and how to use them to evaluate your strategy.

## Equity curve

The equity curve is a line chart showing your portfolio's total value over the backtest period. It is the single most important visualization.

**What to look for:**

- **Smooth upward slope:** The strategy is consistently profitable across different market conditions.
- **Steep drops followed by recovery:** The strategy experiences drawdowns but recovers. Check the depth and duration of drops.
- **Flat sections:** The strategy is inactive (no trades). This could mean your entry conditions are too strict.
- **Steady decline:** The strategy is losing money. Revisit your logic.

A good equity curve rises over time with manageable dips. A great equity curve does so consistently, not just in one lucky period.

## Performance metrics

### Total return

The overall percentage gain or loss from start to finish.

`Total Return = (Final Equity - Starting Capital) / Starting Capital * 100`

A positive return is necessary but not sufficient — you also need to consider *how* the return was achieved (smoothly vs. with wild swings).

### Sharpe ratio

Measures risk-adjusted return. A higher Sharpe means better returns per unit of risk.

| Sharpe ratio | Interpretation                    |
|--------------|-----------------------------------|
| < 0          | Losing money                      |
| 0 - 1.0      | Below average risk-adjusted return |
| 1.0 - 2.0    | Good                              |
| 2.0 - 3.0    | Very good                         |
| > 3.0        | Excellent (verify it's not overfitting) |

> A Sharpe ratio above 3.0 in a backtest often signals overfitting. Be cautious and test across different time periods.

### Max drawdown

The largest peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value during the backtest, expressed as a percentage.

For example, if your equity peaked at $12,000 and then dropped to $9,600 before recovering, the max drawdown is 20%.

A max drawdown of 20-30% is common for active crypto strategies. Above 50% is a serious red flag — most traders cannot psychologically withstand losses that deep.

### Win rate

The percentage of trades that were profitable.

`Win Rate = Winning Trades / Total Trades * 100`

Win rate alone is not meaningful. A strategy with a 30% win rate can be highly profitable if the average win is much larger than the average loss.

### Profit factor

The ratio of gross profits to gross losses.

`Profit Factor = Total Profit from Winners / Total Loss from Losers`

| Profit factor | Interpretation         |
|---------------|------------------------|
| < 1.0         | Losing money overall   |
| 1.0 - 1.5     | Marginal profitability |
| 1.5 - 2.0     | Solid                  |
| > 2.0         | Strong                 |

### Average trade duration

How long the strategy holds a position on average. This helps you understand the trading style:

- **Minutes to hours:** Scalping / intraday
- **Hours to days:** Swing trading
- **Days to weeks:** Position trading

### Total trades

The number of completed round-trip trades. More trades give you more statistical confidence in the results. A backtest with only 5 trades is not reliable, even if they were all winners.

> Aim for at least 30 completed trades to have meaningful statistical significance. If your backtest produces fewer, try extending the date range or using a shorter timeframe.

## Trade log

The trade log lists every order executed during the backtest:

| Column      | Description                           |
|-------------|---------------------------------------|
| Timestamp   | When the order was placed             |
| Side        | Buy or Sell                           |
| Symbol      | Trading pair                          |
| Quantity    | Amount traded                         |
| Price       | Execution price (after slippage)      |
| P&L         | Profit or loss (for sell orders)      |
| Cumulative  | Running total P&L                     |

Use the trade log to spot patterns: Are most losses happening at certain times? Are there clusters of losing trades? Does the strategy overtrade?

## Drawdown chart

The drawdown chart shows how far below the previous equity peak your portfolio has fallen at each point in time. It is the inverse of the equity curve's highs.

**What to look for:**

- **Shallow, short drawdowns:** The strategy recovers quickly. Good sign.
- **Deep, prolonged drawdowns:** The strategy takes a long time to recover from losses. Risky.
- **Drawdowns coinciding with known market events:** Check if your strategy handles crashes and black swan events.

## Comparing strategies

To compare two strategies fairly:

1. Use the **same symbol, timeframe, date range, and starting capital** for both.
2. Enable the **same slippage and commission settings**.
3. Compare Sharpe ratio and max drawdown first, then total return.

A strategy with lower total return but much lower max drawdown may be the better choice for live trading.

> Past backtest results do not guarantee future performance. Backtesting shows how a strategy *would have* performed, not how it *will* perform.
